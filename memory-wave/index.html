The Science Behind Gamma Waves — and Your Brain's Cleaning System

To understand why The Memory Wave is generating interest in the neuroscience community, you need to understand two things: what Gamma brainwaves are, and what the brain's "cleaning system" actually does.

Your brain produces electrical activity across different frequency bands. Alpha waves are associated with relaxation. Theta waves with deep focus and creativity. Delta waves with sleep. But Gamma waves — the fastest frequency, oscillating at 30-100 Hz — are associated with something far more important than any single cognitive function.

The Glymphatic System: Your Brain's Built-In Cleaning Crew

In 2012, researchers at the University of Rochester discovered the glymphatic system — essentially the brain's waste disposal network. This system flushes out metabolic waste products, including the toxic proteins that accumulate with age and are associated with cognitive decline.

What researchers discovered more recently is that Gamma brainwave activity appears to play a direct role in activating and enhancing this cleaning process. In other words, when the brain produces strong Gamma waves, the waste-clearing system works more efficiently.

The Research

A landmark 2019 study published in Nature demonstrated that exposing subjects to 40 Hz Gamma frequency stimulation — through both light and sound — triggered a measurable increase in glymphatic clearance activity. Follow-up studies at MIT and other leading institutions have confirmed and expanded on these findings, showing improvements in neural connectivity, immune response, and cognitive markers.

"The connection between Gamma waves and brain health is one of the most exciting developments in neuroscience in the last decade," says Dr. Emily Lawson, a neurobiologist specializing in age-related cognitive decline. "We're seeing evidence that stimulating these frequencies can actually support the brain's ability to maintain itself — to keep running its own cleaning and maintenance processes as we age."
