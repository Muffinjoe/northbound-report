The Science: Why Falls Really Happen After 60

The conventional wisdom says falls happen because of age-related muscle loss, poor vision, medication side effects, or environmental hazards. And while all of those are contributing factors, Harvard evolutionary biologist Dr. Daniel Lieberman's research points to something more fundamental.

Your feet contain thousands of nerve endings designed to sense the ground beneath you — detecting every subtle change in terrain, angle, and texture. These nerves send signals to your muscles to make instantaneous micro-adjustments that keep you upright. It's your body's own built-in traction control system.

But here's the problem: after a lifetime of wearing shoes, the most critical of these nerves — the Deep Peroneal Nerve — gradually goes dormant. It stops firing the muscles in your foot and lower leg that are responsible for catching you when you stumble.

The 2019 Nature study (Holowka et al.) confirmed this by comparing barefoot populations in Kenya with shoe-wearing populations. The barefoot groups maintained dramatically stronger nerve sensitivity in their feet throughout their entire lives — and had virtually no age-related falls.
